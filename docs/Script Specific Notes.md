  - ***Georgian scripts***: Disputed origin. It seems the majority opinion is that it's derived from Greek, though some argue a Semitic or independent origin. Also one of the toughest for distinguishing what counts as separate scripts. In order, it goes Asomtavruli->Nushkuri->Mkhedruli. However, a combined style of using Asomtavruli as uppercase and Nushkuri as lowercase took hold called Khutsuri (a generally unused version exists for Mkhedruli as well). This parallels a bit the Latin script with cursive forms eventually becoming lowercases. For All Scripts, I ended up putting the three scripts in a single box to show it as stages of a script. For modern I went with Khutsuri and Mkhedruli to show Khutsuri still being used in specialized (religious) use cases.

  - ***Hangul***: Probably the most controversial (partial) derivision I've made on the All Scripts diagram, so I've got the references (in Modern Scripts it is shown as disputed). Basically there seems to be a credible theory that some consonants (and maybe a few vowels) were graphical simplifications of 'Phags-pa consonants, including 4 with the same sound value (a script used by the neighbouring fallen Yuan dynasty elites).
    - *[Script ‘Borrowing’, Cultural Influence and the Development of the Written Vernacular in East Asia](https://www.academia.edu/19593770/Script_Borrowing_Cultural_Influence_and_the_Development_of_the_Written_Vernacular_in_East_Asia)* (Nicolas Tranter, 2001)
    > It has often been pointed out, especially by non-Korean scholars, that *han’gŭl* shows many similarities with *’phags-pa*, leading to Miller’s extreme position that *han’gŭl* was ‘adopted from the Tibetan-Mongolian »universal phonetic alphabet« of Yüan China’ (Miller 1996: 61) or is ‘the Korean version of ḥPhags-pa script’ (ibid: 145). The evidence does support the fact that certain forms of *han’gŭl* are derived from *’phags-pa*, but this is certainly not the whole picture. *han’gŭl* is in many ways a more original—and perhaps ‘scientific’—script than *’phags-pa* ever was.
    - The International Linguistic Background of the Correct Sounds for the Instruction of the People (Gari Ledyard, 1997), a chapter in the book *[The Korean Alphabet: Its History and Structure](https://books.google.ca/books?hl=en&lr=&id=U8ACEQAAQBAJ&oi=fnd&pg=PP1&ots=8cNuBB8wMe&sig=gx5piRLw-oNUS5sTs7bqgthEn2c&redir_esc=y#v=onepage&q&f=false)* (Kim-Renaud & Young-Key, 1997)
         - This one I unfortunately can't find fully outside a paywall, but this seems to be the "canonical" paper on the 'Phags-pa theory that others cite, so you can search on from there. For example, *[漢字無罪: Politics and Divergent Orthography Strategies Following the 'Phags-pa Alphabet](https://www.academia.edu/download/61106594/Low_final_orthography20191102-53012-82gujh.pdf)* (Syd Low) makes the following statement and leaves a citation to it:
         > [...] it would have been unwise for King Sejong to explicitly mention the Phags-pa alphabet as having any relation to *hangul*. But the original explanitory [sic] text for the new script, the *Hunminjeongeum Haerye* 훈민정음 해례 訓民正⾳解例, lit. "Explanations and Examples of the Correct Sounds for the Instruction of the People,” does mention what could be translated as “Old Seal Script,” 古篆字. However because *hangul* does not resemble Chinese seal script, some propose the 古 is instead an abbreviation of 蒙古, Mongol.
    - There's also a Korean source, *[Vowel Letters of Hunmin-Jeongeum and Phags-pa Script](https://www.kci.go.kr/kciportal/landing/article.kci?arti_id=ART001406063)* (정광, 2009) which while I cannot read (both the language and the paywall), the abstract is available in English.

  - **Hieratic**: See also Egyptian Hieroglyphs. There's a distinction between cursive hieroglyphs and Hieratic that not all sources make. Hieratic begins as cursive hieroglyphs, but eventually becomes distinct enough to be considered a writing system in its own right. Due to this, the chart shows Hieratic branching around the time it distinguished itself. Sources say Hieratic started to become distinct in the Middle Kingdom (c. 2050-1650 BCE, or possibly First Intermediate Period prior), becoming fully distinct by the New Kingdom (c. 1550-1069 BCE). Usually with a date range (here, 2050-1550 BCE), I would put the date in the middle, However in this case we have suggestion of it occurring earlier & one feature of Hieratic proper (consistent RTL direction) was in place around the start of the Middle Kingdom, so I've biased the date earlier. Source: *[The Transcription of New Kingdom Hieratic](https://www.jstor.org/stable/3854012?read-now=1&seq=1#page_scan_tab_contents)* (Alan H. Gardiner, 1929).

      - Psalter Pahlavi is primarily known from a single source discovered in modern day Xinjiang, China. A second shorter inscription was discovered in modern day Afghanistan, dating to the 8th century so I've used that as the end date. With the two known sources being in separate regions, for simplicity, I'm going to consider it as likely developing within West Asia then spreading out.
      - [Multiple](https://www.iranicaonline.org/articles/avestan-language/) [sources](https://www.iranicaonline.org/articles/pahlavi-psalter/) have Avestan borrowing a couple letters from Psalter Pahlavi, implying the Psalter script existed before Avestan, so I've made sure the start dates align that way. I've placed Avestan in South Asia, with it [likely originating in the Eastern Persian empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avestan_geography) (modern day Afghanistan, Pakistan and maybe even India).

Cross-referencing the UEP and other sources, it turns out that "Inscriptional Parthian" is frequently just "Parthian" in the literature, while "Inscriptional Pahlavi" can be "Middle Persian" (though care must be taken to ensure that it's the script and not language being referred to and it might be a super-script scenario where all the Pahlavi proper scripts are lumped together). I only found this out at the end of round 2 of research, maybe round 3 will yield more results.

This poses date problems, as just going off of Wikipedia, both Avestan and Book Pahlavi exist *before* the earliest samples of Psalter. Further research will be required. At present, the diagram does not adopt Psalter->Book preferring instead to derive directly from Inscriptional to simplify the dating issue and few sources making the specific derivation.

  - ***Kawi & derived***:
      - Dates: In general, Wikipedia start dates for these scripts were uncited and I had a difficult time trying to verify them. This is an area of the world where the climate and writing materials really do not help in preservation and dates are hard to come by. I was only able to find usable citations for dates for less than half the scripts, the remaining are just lined up with their supposed sibling scripts (where possible, the charts puts same-island scripts next to each other). One of the sources did mention first modern Javanese as being around 1620. Since ite developed gradually from Kawi, I instead put the label earlier to line up with the other Java-area scripts (had a date for Old Sundanese in the sources).
      - ***Indonesian scripts***: Best I can tell, the usual order of Kawi script introduction is Java (and Bali)->Sumatra->Sulawesi->Philippines, though with both Sumatra & Sulawesi potentially being in parallel with the preceding step. Some sources make distinction between Old Balinese / Old Javanese / Old Sumatran, but at best these seem to be picking at details and we are still dealing with a more-or-less unified Kawi script at that stage. So in the All Scripts chart, excepting the Philippines scripts, these are all shown as deriving directly from Kawi for simplicity.
          - The Sulawesi scripts (Lontara/Bugis & Makasar) are possibly Ulu-derived (Sumatra) and may not even be directly related to each other.  Further the naming can get confusing. Bugis is also sometimes referred to as Makasar in the literature, and in this context the other Makasar script is referred to as Old Makasar. This is confusing as it is possible the Old Makasar script is a descendent and not ancestor of Bugis/Makasar! The naming is from the point of view of the Makasar people, as their original Old Makasar script got supplanted by the Bugis script.
      - ***Baybayin***: Many theories on origin, All Scripts shows this as derived from Lontara / Bugis. All current theories except Sulawesi run into the problem of final consonants: Baybayin and the rest of the Philippines scripts do not traditionally write syllable-final consonants (some have introduced a mechanism in modern times), so why would it remove that ability from a script that had it? Sulawesi origin does not run into this problem as the Sulawesi languages make minimal use of final consonants. On the other theories:
          - There is a Cham theory which I have no further comment on.
          - There is a Gujarati theory, which the paper advancing it says is unlikely to be direct without first going through Sulawesi (the paper more directly puts the Gujarati origin on the Sulawesi and Sumatran scripts).
          - There is the direct Kawi theory which is mostly evidenced by a few early Kawi samples on the islands. However, oral history reported from Spanish contact (mid 16th c.) has the indigenous people believing that their script was recent, so unlikely to have been maintained from the early Kawi.

Based on dates matching in Wikipedia, so assuming Prachalit to be the original which Ranjana derives from. [The Indo-Aryan Languages](https://books.google.ca/books?id=J3RSHWePhXwC) (Colin P. Masica, 1993) p. 43 has the script deriving from proto-Bengali aka Gaudi. There's a fairly solid dating of the script to 908 in a Buddhist sutra. However, [Indian Epigraphy](https://archive.org/details/IndianEpigraphy/mode/2up) (Richard Saloman, 1998) p. 42 has Gaudi as starting in the tenth century, which generally wouldn't be enough time to differentiate. So one of three things must be true: They aren't that different, the cited derivation is incorrect, or Gaudi should be dated earlier.
  - **Northern Nagari**: This is the compromise attempt for the varying definitions that seem to be floating around. The Unicode encoding proposal for Nandinagari is so far the only source I've found which mentions all three of Nandinagari, Nagari and Devanagari as being distinct things, but then also immediately implies Nagari and Devanagari to not be that different. So the diagram shows the split between Northern and South/Central Nagaris (the latter is Nandinagari). Then Northern Nagari is shown in the same box as Devanagari to acknowledge that they are effectively development of the same script (with it non-italicized to indicate it should be treated as a font variant of Devanagari, though still debating this whole font-variant indication). The splits to other scripts are shown before the Devanagari label to allow for them to derive off of plain Nagari for the many sources that indicate that (though for what it's worth, Daniel's The [World's Writing Systems](https://archive.org/details/isbn_9780195079937/page/390/mode/2up) calls Gujarati a Devanagari variant).
  - **Nushkuri**: See Georgian.
  - **Nushu**: Just about the least certain for origin date. There's broad agreement peak usage was late Qing (c. 1900), but origin is totally unknown, with one source even having suggested Song dynasty (960-1279). Early dates are mostly speculation, with actual writings dating to late nineteeth century, so the diagram biases the date towards the later end. Sources:
      - *["My Mother Watched Over an Empty House and was Separated From the Heavenly Female": Nushu and the Writing IOf Religious Culture in China](https://prod-ms-be.lib.mcmaster.ca/server/api/core/bitstreams/a280cd09-2c4c-4356-8908-32d24f0021f3/content)* (Stephanie Balkwill, 2006).
      - *[History, Characteristics, and Modern Vitality of Nüshu: A Cultural Anthropology Perspective](https://www.academia.edu/download/111888778/Anthropological_Explorations_2024.pdf#page=98)* (Li Congrong, 2024)
  - ***Nyiakeng Puachue Hmong***: Several letters similar to Hebrew while appearing Thai or Lao in style. Seems reasonable for a script devised by a Hmong Christian group. Far from definitive, so I put disputed arrows.
  - ***Pahawh Hmong***, Appears mainly an independent invention, though significantly influenced by Lao. There appears to be some influence from Pollard and Fraser as well, but since that is lesser and it would be a mess trying to incoporate that, I didn't. Also, unlike the other abugidas, it seems this one's vowel-based.
  - ***Pallava***: Derived from the Tamil-Brahmi variant of Brahmi. Dating is tight here with a vague "4th century" start date, see Cham.
  - **Palmyrene**: See also Manichaean.
  - ***Phoenician***: Per [Merlo](https://mnamon.sns.it/index.php?page=Scrittura&id=53&lang=en), the diagram adopts the distinction between Proto-Canaanite & Phoenician as stabilized writing direction and letter inventory. [Proto-Canaanite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Canaanite_alphabet) has two separate nominal definitions of the ancestor to Phoenician, and the regional form of Proto-Sinaitic in Canaan. Reading in-between the lines, this may in fact ultimately be a singular unbroken Proto-Canaanite, but we are simply lacking archaelogical records (allowing for proper Proto-Sinaitic exist alongside). Generally then, this diagram would put these scripts in a singular script box for Proto-Sinaitic->Proto-Canaanite->Phoenician, but breaks it up to account for region. The singular box also allows being a bit ambiguous about Paleo-Hebrew which sources have as variously deriving from Phoenician and Proto-Canaanite. Sources:
      - *[The Alphabet Comes of Age The Social Context of Alphabetic Writing in the First Millennium BCE](https://www.academia.edu/39784078/_The_Alphabet_Comes_of_Age_The_Social_Context_of_Alphabetic_Writing_in_the_First_Millennium_BCE)* (Christopher Rollston, 2017).
      - *[Much ado about an implement! – the Phoenicianising of Early Alphabetic](https://library.oapen.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.12657/51112/1/UnderstandingrelationsFinal%20Proofs.pdf#page=61)* (Reinhard G. Lehmann, 2020).
      - *[The Ancient Languages of Syria-Palestine and Arabia](https://books.google.ca/books?id=vTrT-bZyuPcC&pg=PA4&redir_esc=y#v=onepage&q&f=false)* (Roger D. Woodard, 2008) which this diagram completely aligns with
  - ***Pollard Miao***: While a derivation from (Cree) Canadian Aboriginal Syllabics is often mentioned, this derivation is currently not included. At present, analysis from the beta database project puts the percentage of derived letters at just under 20%. That said, minor data changes could flip that so it's worth discussing further. I am personally skeptical of a direct graphical symbol derivation, though the two main sources I could find ultimately do make this link (I would be happy to be proved wrong, I am Canadian and would like this to be true). Pollard was aware of the syllabics. But did he have access to the specific symbols? As an armchair historian my personal take is that based on the script, it seems quite likely he followed many of the same principles: Finals being written smaller, using symbol rotation and using simple symbols to increase learn-ability, in particular by drawing on Pitman Shorthand. But none of this implies symbol borrowing, in particular because simple symbols are easy to independently derive (eg. Pollard could have easily rotated latin L himself). Conversely, the seemingly less mentioned Pitman derivation is made on the chart. The database has it at about 23.3% so would take at least a few letter derivation changes to remove this. While still no direct symbol link I'm aware of, Pollard himself explicitly wrote of stuctural Pitman influence in the tone position system. Of note, none of supposedly Cree-derived symbols match in sound value, while potentially Pitman derived n & m originally did (they later got rotated 90 degrees). Sources:
      - *[A Myth Become Reality: History and Development of the Miao Written Language](https://www.diva-portal.org/smash/get/diva2:796302/FULLTEXT02)* (Joakim Enwall, 1994)
      - *[Two related Indigenous writing systems: Canada's syllabic and China's A-Hmao scripts'](https://syllabics.net/files/cree/two_related_indigenous_writing_systems.pdf)* (Lewis & Dorais, 2003)

  - ***Proto-Sinaitic***: See also Hieratic & Phoenician. Very few inscriptions survive to the modern day, making relationships a bit tentative. Proto-Sinaitic branches of Egyptian writing early enough that it seems unlikely to have branched from Hieratic proper, though may very well have branched from cursive hieroglyphs. I'm making a bit of an assumption on the writing direction based on Egyptian Hieroglyphs. Source (unrelated to the hieratic/cursive): *[Ancient Egypt and the earliest known stages of alphabetic writing](https://library.oapen.org/bitstream/handle/20.500.12657/51112/1/UnderstandingrelationsFinal%20Proofs.pdf#page=61)* (Ben Haring, 2020).

  - ***Rejang***: See Ulu.
  - ***Sinhala***: More research needed. Finding detailed credible sources on this has been tricky. Mostly they agree on Sinhala initially being a separate development of original Brahmi. The two main stories I've seen is that this Old Sinhala was not continued and a new one developed from Pallava/Grantha (what the charts currently show) or that from initial Northern development it became heavily modified with surrounding Southern script influence.
  - **Sogdian**: Wikipedia (uncited) and Daniels & Bright have derivision from Syriac. However, most other sources derive directly from Aramaic. Given that and the fact Syriac isn't really a minor script that I would expect sources to skip, Aramaic is chosen. Taking the start date to be 313 per the "Ancient Letters", the Kultobe inscription is assumed to be earlier based on older letter forms - which seems to imply a transitional phase between the scripts. Sources:

  - ***Tai scripts***: These were hard to research.
      - ***Tai Tham***: As I write this, Wikipedia has it deriving from specifically Old Mon with 3 citations. But one seems to not be in the citation given, one's behind a paywall, and the other seems to say the opposite, that it's a later borrowing. So I'm not too worried about deriving it specifically to Old Mon, and Mon-Burmese is fine.
      - ***Tai Viet,*** **Tai Yo**: There's a theory that a "proto-Thai" script existed that was the progenitor of Sukhothai and the Khmer-derived Tai scripts. Lacking firm evidence, the All Scirpts diagram have these separately derived from Khmer. Alternative theory is that it's a later derivation from Sukhothai (though if I understand the languages/scripts at the time correctly, to me this seems less likely as why would the Tai drop Thai tone markers?). Note that Unicode's Tai Viet is a unification of Tai Dam (Black Tai), Tai Don (White Tai) and Thai Song. UEP mentions Tai Daeng (Red Tai) is similar, but will probably require it's own encoding. Sources:
          - *[Sur l'ancienneté des écritures thai d'origine indo-khmère](https://shs.hal.science/halshs-00922719)* (Michel Ferlus, 1999)

      - **Lik Tai scripts**: Mostly relying on *[Script without Buddhism: Burmese Influence on the Tay (Shan) Sript of Mäng Maaw as seen in a Chinese Scroll Painting of 1407](https://www.academia.edu/download/106950535/s147959141200001020231026-1-q0d1ft.pdf)* (Christian Daniels, 2012) and *[Historical Evidence for the Early Lik Tai Scripts](https://www.academia.edu/40578293/Historical_Evidence_for_the_Early_Lik_Tai_Scripts)* (David Wharton, 2019). These are scripts with unclear relationships. Identification of Tai in 2012 of a 1407 scroll should by default override prior research. Previously, Lik Tho Ngok was assumed to be an early pregenitor ([aka Tai Le aka Dehong Dai](https://www.omniglot.com/writing/dehongdai.htm)). That scroll is however much more similar to Tai Ahom. Lik Tho Ngok samples are apparently absent till after 18th century which further counters the traditional narrative. Regionally, [Mäng Maaw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6ng_Mao) straddles South, Southeast and East Asia making the All Scripts diagram really tricky (the capital even spanned the border of modern day Myanmar & China). Given likely derivation of Lik Tai from Mon-Burmese, I'm placing it in SE Asia, given contact would have been from the South. Importantly, no one's calling the 1407 text Ahom itself, just similar. Ahom first shows up in [India in 1532](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sadiya_Serpent_Pillar) (it's great when the inscription includes a date), so I assume some prior proto-Lik-Tai script exists before this (Wharton says starting sometime between 12th-14th c.).
  - ***Thaana***: Uniquely, the letters are derived from (Eastern) Arabic and Indic numeral systems (the local Dhives Akuru numerals). Numbers aren't generally considered a language so their representation aren't considered a writing system to be included in this diagram. Including numerals would cause a conundrum, as they did not always develop in the same way as associated scripts. In particular, the generally internationally recognized 0-9 came from India through (Western) Arabic intermediary. Would that mean all scripts using these digits be partially Arabic derived? This would make the chart unreadable, thus, for consistency, Thaana gets the independent group in these diagrams for turning numeric symbols into letters.

  - ***Tifinagh***: Marginal inclusion for both Tifinagh or Neo-Tifinagh on Modern Scripts diagram. Frequently sources would just use Tifinagh for the modern version, but sources that distinguished did tend to say Tifinagh had some limited use. I believe the confusion may lie in how related these scripts are. Libyco-Berber is a group of related scripts, with Tifinagh emerging as one, then Neo-Tifinagh was a standardization with vowels added (if I've understood correctly). Neo-Tifinagh was developed at a Berber academy in Europe, but I've opted not to branch it on the All Scripts diagram as it seems to be a small evolution. Source: *[A Saharan archive and its afterlives](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10502-025-09482-0)* (Fiona McLaughlin, 2025).

North Brahmi is chosen for both geographic reasons and more similar to geometric Asokan Brahmi associated to the North. Source: *[Schrift und Sprache in den Werken Lako Bodras im Gebiet der Ho von Singblum (Bihar)](https://www.jstor.org/stable/40459537)* (Heinz-Jürgen Pinnow, 1972).
  - **Yezidi**: Few sources. Daniels & Bright has a stub entry stating it to be potentially Arabic & Latin derived and potentially a cipher. [Yezidi Alphabet: The Way to Unicode](https://journals.org.ge/index.php/asianstudies/article/view/152) (Rovenchak, Pirbari & Karaca, 2023) only mentions Arabic.
